## 1. Recommended Board Composition

### Ideal Size: 5-7 Members

**Why 5-7:**
- Fewer than 3 limits breadth of perspective and looks thin
- More than 9 becomes unwieldy and signals optics over substance
- 5-7 is the consensus for early-stage legal tech — large enough for diverse expertise, small enough for genuine engagement
- For access-to-justice legal tech specifically, regulatory complexity (UPL risk, bar association politics, court system partnerships) demands advisors who are deeply engaged, not loosely affiliated

### The Six Seats Framework

Based on cross-referencing successful legal tech advisory boards (JTA, IAALS, Clio, Rocket Lawyer) with justack.ai's specific access-to-justice mission:

| Seat | Role | Why Critical for Justack.ai |
|------|------|-----------------------------|
| **1. Regulatory/UPL Expert** | Practicing attorney or former bar counsel who understands unauthorized practice of law boundaries | Existential risk — DoNotPay's $193K FTC settlement shows what happens without this. Ensures justack.ai stays on the right side of UPL in Ontario and BC |
| **2. Legal Aid / A2J Domain Expert** | Someone from legal services ecosystem — legal aid, court self-help, or SRLN | Ensures you're solving real problems for real users, not building "legal tech for lawyers" |
| **3. Technology / AI Leader** | CTO or VP Engineering from a scaled SaaS company, ideally in regulated industries | Guides AI implementation, platform scalability, and technical credibility |
| **4. Indigenous Law Expert** | Indigenous law practitioner or scholar with governance board experience | Critical for Canadian A2J credibility, reconciliation lens, and serving Indigenous communities |
| **5. Legal Tech Founder/Entrepreneur** | Someone who has built and scaled a legal tech company | Provides operational credibility, fundraising guidance, and market validation |
| **6. Consumer/Public Interest Advocate** | Consumer rights advocate, social worker, or community organizer representing end users | Most often missing from legal tech boards — its absence is the leading cause of A2J product-market fit failures |

**Optional 7th seat:** Fundraising/impact investor specialist if pursuing venture or grant funding.

## 2. Best Practices for Unpaid Advisory Boards

### Structure

| Element | Recommendation |
|---------|---------------|
| **Meeting frequency** | Quarterly (4x/year). Less loses continuity; more exhausts goodwill |
| **Meeting length** | 60-90 minutes virtual; 2-3 hours in-person |
| **Term length** | 1 year, renewable by mutual agreement. Natural exit ramp without awkwardness |
| **Time commitment** | 2-5 hours/month total (prep + meeting + ad hoc) |
| **Communication** | Brief email update between meetings; availability for occasional ad hoc calls |

### Written Agreement (Even Without Compensation)

Even uncompensated advisory boards need a written agreement. Key elements:

1. **Role definition** — Advisory (non-fiduciary). No voting rights, no governance authority, no binding decisions
2. **Scope of advisory services** — Which domains (strategy, regulatory, technology, etc.)
3. **Time commitment** — Expected hours, meeting frequency
4. **Term and termination** — Start date, term, mutual 30-day termination notice
5. **Confidentiality** — Advisors will see sensitive business information
6. **Conflict of interest disclosure** — Must disclose competing relationships
7. **No agency** — Cannot bind the company or represent themselves as agents
8. **Indemnification** — Company indemnifies advisors for good-faith actions

### Anti-Patterns to Avoid

Research identified these common failures:

1. **Name collecting** — Optimizing for titles rather than relevant expertise and actual availability
2. **No structure** — No meeting cadence, no expectations, no defined asks
3. **Treating advisors as employees** — Expecting 10+ hours/month from uncompensated members
4. **No exit mechanism** — Making it awkward to transition advisors out when fit degrades
5. **Ignoring input** — Asking for advice and never acting on it (fastest way to lose advisors)
6. **Homogeneous composition** — All lawyers, no tech; all academics, no practitioners

## 3. What Makes an Advisory Board Credible

### To Investors/VCs

| Signal | Why It Works |
|--------|-------------|
| Serial founders with exits | Proves they've navigated the journey before |
| Active executives (not retired) | Current networks, current market knowledge |
| Domain-specific operators | Reduces market risk perception |
| Functional expertise gaps filled | Shows founders know what they don't know |
| Evidence of engagement | Vesting schedules, meeting cadence, documented contributions |

**Warning:** Sophisticated investors see bloated advisory boards as a red flag. Quality over quantity. 5 engaged advisors > 15 names on a page.

### To Practicing Lawyers

| Signal | Why It Works |
|--------|-------------|
| Former bar association leaders | Highest institutional credibility |
| Former/retired judges | Embodies the justice system |
| Legal Services Corporation / Legal Aid leaders | Bridges A2J credibility |
| Law school innovation directors | Academic legitimacy + forward-thinking |
| ABA/CBA-recognized innovators | Professional endorsement signal |

**Key insight:** An advisory board with respected lawyers signals to bar associations that justack.ai takes UPL concerns seriously. This has quasi-regulatory function beyond pure credibility.

### To Consumers/Public

| Signal | Why It Works |
|--------|-------------|
| Former AG or senior government official | You already have this — Michael himself |
| Former judges | Trust in the justice system |
| Consumer advocacy background | "Someone is looking out for me" signal |
| Indigenous representation | Signals inclusive, community-first approach |
| Diversity of backgrounds | Reflects the communities served |

### JUSTACK.AI — Advisory Board

**About Justack.ai**
Justack.ai is a legal technology platform focused on access to justice, founded by Michael Bryant (former Attorney General of Ontario, CEO of the Canadian Civil Liberties Association, and CEO of Legal Aid BC). Our mission is to bridge the justice gap by using AI to help Canadians understand their legal rights and navigate the legal system.

**Advisory Board Purpose**
The Advisory Board provides strategic guidance and credibility to justack.ai as we build a platform serving Canadians who cannot afford or access traditional legal services. Board members lend their expertise and reputation to a mission-driven legal technology company at the intersection of AI, law, and access to justice.

**Role Description**
Advisory Board members:
- Provide strategic counsel on legal technology, access to justice, and regulatory matters
- Offer perspective from their domain of expertise when consulted
- Allow their name and affiliation to be listed on the justack.ai website and materials
- Make introductions to relevant contacts when appropriate and comfortable
- Serve as a sounding board for key decisions at quarterly meetings

**What This Is Not**
- Not a board of directors — no fiduciary duties, no voting rights, no governance authority
- Not a full-time or part-time commitment — this is a light-touch advisory role
- Not compensated — this is a volunteer/pro-bono contribution to access to justice

**Time Commitment**
- **Quarterly meetings:** 60-90 minutes, virtual (4x per year)
- **Ad hoc consultation:** Occasional emails or brief calls as needed (estimated 1-2 hours between meetings)
- **Total:** Approximately 2-4 hours per quarter

**Term**
- Initial term: 1 year
- Renewable by mutual agreement
- Either party may step back with 30 days' notice — no hard feelings

**What We Provide**
- Brief written update before each quarterly meeting
- Recognition on justack.ai website and materials
- The satisfaction of contributing to meaningful access-to-justice innovation
- A confidentiality agreement protecting sensitive business information shared during advisory work

## 7. Diversity Considerations

Research findings on advisory board diversity specific to legal tech:

- **A2J-focused legal tech companies show 44% diverse founders** vs. 26.5% industry average — companies solving problems for diverse communities benefit from diverse leadership
- **Non-diverse teams designing A2J technology are "highly susceptible to design bias"** — this is a product-quality argument, not just an optics argument
- **Canada's CBCA identifies four groups for board diversity:** women, Indigenous peoples, persons with disabilities, and visible minorities
- **Indigenous representation on legal tech advisory boards has no published data** — being proactive here is both right and differentiating

The recommended 5-person configuration above achieves strong diversity across gender, Indigenous representation, and professional background without making diversity the sole organizing principle.
