## I Fired My Bookkeeper This Week

Not out of frustration with her. Out of recognition that the job no longer needs to exist.

I was paying between three and six thousand dollars a year for bookkeeping and accounting services. QuickBooks was the centerpiece — a tool I found unintuitive and inefficient, which meant I was paying someone else to operate software I didn't understand, to produce reports I only saw at tax time.

This week, I replaced the entire stack. My financial records are now plain text files — a format called beancount — that my AI assistant reads and writes directly via an open protocol called MCP (Model Context Protocol). Bank transactions get downloaded as CSVs, categorized by AI in seconds, and written into version-controlled ledger files. When I want to know what I spent on software last quarter, I ask. The answer comes back in under a second. When tax season arrives, a script generates the exact category totals my CPA needs.

Total annual software cost: zero dollars. CPA engagement: tax prep only, scoped down from a full-service relationship to a few hundred dollars for review and filing.

This is not a story about bookkeeping. This is a story about what happens when software stops being a product and starts being a protocol.

## The Great Transition

In February 2026, Daniel Miessler published a piece called "[The Great Transition](https://danielmiessler.com/blog/the-great-transition)" that articulated something many of us have been sensing but hadn't yet named. His thesis: multiple simultaneous transitions are converging into one great shift that will make traditional packaged software obsolete.

"Software goes from everyone using the same packages to everyone getting bespoke software," he writes. The old model — build the app, design the UI, package it, market it — is collapsing. In its place: APIs and MCP endpoints that AI agents consume directly, generating custom workflows on demand.

"If I have to open an app, I've already lost," Miessler says. "My AI should be doing all of this for me."

The implications for consumer software are already visible. But the implications for professional services — and for law in particular — are staggering and largely unexamined. What happens when the law firm itself becomes an API?

## Five Layers of the API-Native Law Firm

What follows is an architecture — not a prediction, but a design. Some of these pieces exist today. Others need to be built. All of them are technically feasible with current or near-current technology. I've organized them as layers, each building on the one below.

### Layer 1: The Back Office Vanishes

This is the layer that's already happening. It's what I did with my own bookkeeping this week. Every administrative function in a law firm can be decomposed into discrete tasks, and nearly all of them are automatable:

**Billing and invoicing.** AI generates invoices from time entries or fixed-scope engagement schedules, sends them, tracks payment, follows up. No human touches it unless a dispute arises.

**Conflict checks.** When a prospective matter arrives, AI searches the firm's entire client and matter database, cross-references adverse parties, identifies potential conflicts, and flags those requiring lawyer review. What currently takes a paralegal 30 minutes takes AI 3 seconds.

**Document assembly.** Engagement letters, NDAs, standard agreements — all templated, populated from client data, and presented for lawyer review rather than lawyer drafting.

**Compliance and deadlines.** Limitation periods, filing deadlines, regulatory submission dates — tracked, calendared, and escalated automatically. No tickler system. No "I forgot." The AI doesn't forget.

**Accounting.** As I've demonstrated with my own practice: AI-categorized transactions, real-time P&L, automated CPA handoff. The bookkeeper role, for small to mid-size firms, ceases to exist.

This layer alone eliminates 40–60% of a typical firm's non-lawyer labor. But it's table stakes. The interesting layers come next.

### Layer 2: Client Engagement via API

Here is where Miessler's thesis gets genuinely radical when applied to law.

Today, engaging a lawyer works like this: you call, email, or fill out a web form. Someone at the firm triages your inquiry. You have a consultation. You sign an engagement letter. Weeks may pass. The process is high-friction, opaque, and designed around the firm's convenience, not the client's.

In the API-native model, client engagement becomes programmable.

**The preferred vendor model.** A corporate client maintains a list of approved law firms, each of which has registered its capabilities, jurisdictions, rate structures, and availability as API endpoints. When a legal need arises — a contract review, a regulatory question, a litigation risk — the client's AI queries the preferred vendor APIs, filters by capability, and initiates engagement.

**Auction and RFP at machine speed.** For non-routine work, the client's system broadcasts a structured RFP to qualified firms. Firms' AI systems assess the matter, run conflict checks, evaluate capacity, and return bids — all within minutes, not weeks. The client's AI evaluates bids on cost, expertise match, track record, and availability. A human decision-maker reviews the shortlist and selects.

**Ongoing advisory as a live connection.** For retained counsel, the relationship is not periodic meetings and email chains. It's a persistent API connection. The client's transaction systems share relevant data in real time — a proposed acquisition, a new vendor contract, a regulatory filing. The firm's AI monitors for legal risk, flags issues, and escalates to a human lawyer only when the risk profile demands it.

This isn't science fiction. It's the logical consequence of Miessler's observation that "businesses become APIs." The law firm becomes a legal intelligence endpoint.

### Layer 3: Token Economics for Legal Services

The billable hour has been under attack for decades. Alternative fee arrangements exist but remain clunky — negotiated case-by-case, tracked manually, reconciled by humans. The API-native model enables something cleaner.

**Subscription tiers with token budgets.** A client subscribes at a tier that includes a monthly allocation of "legal tokens" — units of AI-assisted legal work. A contract review might cost 50 tokens. A risk assessment on a proposed transaction, 200 tokens. A brief legal memo, 150. These are consumed automatically as the client's AI queries the firm's API.

**Metered overages with caps.** Beyond the subscription allocation, additional work is metered per-token but capped at a monthly maximum — protecting the client from bill shock while ensuring the firm is compensated for genuine demand spikes.

**Human escalation pricing.** When the AI determines that human lawyer involvement is required — due to complexity, risk level, or novelty — the engagement transitions to a traditional fee structure: fixed fee per matter, or negotiated based on the specific scope. This transition is explicit, documented, and triggered by defined criteria, not by the firm's discretion.

The economic model works because the AI handles 70–85% of routine legal work at near-zero marginal cost. The firm's revenue comes from the subscription base (predictable, recurring) plus human-escalation fees (high-margin, justified by genuine complexity). The client's cost drops by 40–70% compared to traditional hourly billing because most of what they were paying for — research, drafting, review of routine matters — is now automated.

### Layer 4: The Confidentiality Stack

This is where most AI-in-law discussions wave their hands. I won't.

Legal services are subject to some of the most stringent confidentiality requirements in any profession. Solicitor-client privilege, attorney-client privilege (in the US), professional secrecy (in civil law jurisdictions) — these are not suggestions. They are legal obligations enforced by regulators who can disbar practitioners for violations.

When client data flows through API endpoints and AI systems, the confidentiality architecture becomes critical infrastructure, not an afterthought.

**Encrypted MCP.** The Model Context Protocol, as it exists today, does not have a native encryption layer for data in transit between AI systems. This must be built. For legal applications, MCP connections between client and firm systems need end-to-end encryption with mutual authentication — the client's AI must verify it is communicating with the firm's authorized system, and vice versa. This is analogous to mTLS (mutual Transport Layer Security) but at the protocol level of AI-to-AI communication. It does not exist yet. It needs to.

**Blockchain audit trail.** Not blockchain as cryptocurrency hype. Blockchain as immutable, timestamped, distributed ledger for documenting: who accessed what client data, when, through which system, and for what purpose. Legal regulators require firms to demonstrate that confidential information was not improperly accessed or disclosed. An append-only cryptographic ledger provides this proof in a way that traditional access logs — editable, deletable, centralized — cannot.

**Data sovereignty and residency.** Client data processed by AI must respect jurisdictional data residency requirements. A Canadian client's privileged communications cannot be processed on servers subject to US CLOUD Act jurisdiction without informed consent. The API-native firm must offer data residency guarantees as part of its service specification — and its AI infrastructure must enforce them technically, not just contractually.

**Zero-knowledge proof for conflict checks.** When a firm runs a conflict check against a new prospective client, it must compare the new matter against all existing clients and matters. But the prospective client's identity and matter details are themselves confidential until engagement is confirmed. Zero-knowledge cryptographic proofs allow a conflict check to return a yes/no answer without revealing the underlying data to either party. This technology exists in cryptography but has not yet been applied to legal conflicts. It should be.

I raise these points not to suggest that all of them are solved, but to insist that they must be solved — and that any serious proposal for AI-native legal practice must contend with them specifically rather than gesturing vaguely at "security."

### Layer 5: The Human-in-Loop Cascade

This is the layer that answers the question every lawyer asks: "So where do the lawyers go?"

They don't go anywhere. They move up.

In the API-native firm, the AI handles the first pass of everything: initial research, draft documents, risk assessments, contract reviews, compliance checks. But every piece of AI output is subject to human review — and the level of review cascades based on risk and complexity.

**Tier 1: AI autonomous.** Routine, low-risk tasks where the firm has high confidence in AI accuracy. Examples: standard NDA review against the firm's template, initial conflict checks, deadline calculations, client intake data validation. The AI acts, logs its work, and a human reviews the log periodically — like an audit, not an approval.

**Tier 2: Junior lawyer review.** Moderate-complexity tasks where AI produces a draft and a junior lawyer reviews, edits, and approves. Examples: contract markup on a negotiated agreement, legal research memos, risk assessments on routine transactions. The junior lawyer's job shifts from drafting to quality assurance.

**Tier 3: Senior lawyer review.** High-complexity or high-stakes matters where a senior practitioner must evaluate the AI's analysis and the junior's review. Examples: opinions on novel legal questions, strategy recommendations for litigation, advice with significant financial exposure.

**Tier 4: Partner/senior partner decision.** Matters involving firm risk, ethical judgment calls, client relationship decisions, or matters where the AI has flagged genuine uncertainty. The partner does not draft. The partner does not research. The partner evaluates, decides, and takes responsibility.

The cascade is triggered by defined criteria: dollar amount at risk, legal novelty score (assessed by AI), regulatory sensitivity, client relationship tier, and matter type. These triggers are configurable per client, per practice area, and per jurisdiction.

The critical insight is this: **every layer of human involvement is now an audit function, not a production function.** Lawyers review AI work. They don't produce the first draft. This transforms the economics, the staffing model, and — most importantly — the quality. A junior lawyer who reviews 20 AI-drafted contracts per day develops judgment faster than one who drafts 3 from scratch.

## The Regulatory Reality

I would be dishonest — and worse, I would be unhelpful — if I presented this architecture without acknowledging the regulatory barriers that stand between here and there.

**Unauthorized practice of law.** Every jurisdiction defines what constitutes the "practice of law" and restricts it to licensed practitioners. If an AI provides legal analysis directly to a client without lawyer involvement, it may constitute unauthorized practice. The cascade model addresses this by ensuring human lawyers remain in the loop — but regulators will need to update their frameworks to distinguish between AI-generated legal analysis (a tool) and AI-provided legal advice (a service).

**Duty of competence.** Lawyers have an ethical obligation to competently supervise the tools they use. As AI handles more of the substantive work, the profession must define what competent supervision of AI looks like. Is reviewing AI output sufficient? Must the lawyer understand how the AI reached its conclusion? These questions are unresolved.

**Confidentiality rules.** As discussed in Layer 4, existing confidentiality obligations apply to AI systems as they apply to human staff. Regulators in most jurisdictions have not yet issued guidance on AI-specific confidentiality requirements. Some have begun — the Canadian Bar Association and the American Bar Association have published discussion papers — but binding rules are nascent.

**Cross-border practice.** The API-native model naturally crosses jurisdictional boundaries. A client in Ontario queries a firm's API; the firm's AI is trained on Alberta law; the server processing the request is in Virginia. Whose rules apply? Multi-jurisdictional practice rules were designed for humans traveling between offices, not for AI systems that exist everywhere simultaneously.

These are not reasons to abandon the vision. They are engineering constraints — problems to be solved, not walls to be accepted.

## What We're Building

At [justack.ai](https://justack.ai), we're building toward this future. Not all at once — the layers described above represent a 5–10 year architecture. But the foundation is being laid now: open-source legal technology infrastructure that treats the law firm as a system to be designed, not a tradition to be preserved.

The justice stack — the layered architecture of legal service delivery — must be rebuilt from the protocol layer up. Not as another SaaS product that lawyers are forced to adopt and clients are forced to navigate. As infrastructure. As APIs. As endpoints that AI agents consume and humans govern.

The lawyers don't disappear. The friction disappears. The opacity disappears. The $500-per-hour barrier between ordinary people and competent legal help — that disappears.

And what's left is what should have been there all along: human judgment, applied at the right level, to the right problems, at a cost that doesn't exclude 80% of the population from access to justice.

## Coda

I started this piece talking about my bookkeeper. I ended up describing the law firm of 2030. The connection is not accidental.

When I replaced my accounting stack with plain text files and an AI that reads them directly via MCP, I wasn't just saving money. I was experiencing, in miniature, the thesis that Miessler articulated: software as product is giving way to software as protocol. The application layer is dissolving. What remains is data, APIs, and intelligence.

Every profession will go through this. Law is not exempt. The question is not whether, but who builds the architecture — and whether it's built to serve the profession's convenience or the public's need for justice.
