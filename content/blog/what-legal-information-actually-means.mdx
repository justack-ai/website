## The Line That Defines Everything

In Canada, the unauthorized practice of law (UPL) is a regulatory boundary with real consequences. Every province prohibits non-lawyers from practising law. The penalties range from fines to imprisonment. And yet millions of Canadians cannot afford a lawyer. They need help. The question is: what kind of help is permissible?

The answer hinges on a distinction that sounds simple but is not: **legal information versus legal advice.**

## Legal Information

Legal information is general. It describes what the law says, how legal processes work, and what options are typically available. It does not apply the law to a specific person's specific facts. It does not recommend a course of action.

Examples of legal information:

- "In Ontario, the standard notice period for residential tenancies is 60 days."
- "Employment standards legislation sets minimum requirements for termination pay."
- "Small claims court in British Columbia handles disputes up to $5,000."

These are statements of fact about the legal system. Anyone can provide them. Libraries provide them. Government websites provide them. Legal information hotlines provide them. No law society authorization is required.

## Legal Advice

Legal advice is specific. It applies the law to a particular person's circumstances and recommends what that person should do. It requires professional judgment. It creates a duty of care.

Examples of legal advice:

- "Based on your employment contract and the facts you've described, you should reject this severance offer."
- "You have strong grounds for a human rights complaint and should file within 12 months."
- "Your landlord's notice is defective; you do not need to move out."

In Canada, only lawyers and licensed paralegals (in jurisdictions like Ontario where paralegals are regulated) may provide legal advice. The Law Society of Ontario, the Law Society of British Columbia, and their counterparts across the country enforce this boundary.

## Why the Distinction Matters for Technology

Every AI tool that operates in the legal domain must navigate this line. The temptation is to blur it — to provide advice-like outputs wrapped in information-like disclaimers. This is neither honest nor safe.

justack.ai takes a different approach. Our open source tools provide **navigational guidance**: they help people understand what area of law applies to their situation, what processes exist, what documents they might need, and where to find a lawyer or legal aid if they need one. They describe the terrain. They do not tell you which path to take.

This is a deliberate design constraint, not a limitation. The Canadian law society frameworks — particularly the Law Society of Ontario's guidance on UPL and the Federation of Law Societies of Canada's model rules — draw a clear line. Technology that respects that line can operate at scale. Technology that crosses it cannot, and should not.

## The Navigator Model

Think of it as the difference between a map and a guide. A map shows you all the roads, marks the destinations, and notes the hazards. A guide takes your hand, chooses the route, and walks with you. Both are valuable. But only one requires a professional licence.

justack.ai builds maps. When a person needs a guide — and many do — we help them find one. Our lawyer referral tools, our integration with flat-fee legal services, and our directory of legal aid resources exist precisely for this purpose.

## The Gap This Fills

The access-to-justice crisis in Canada is not primarily a shortage of legal advice. It is a shortage of **legal information delivered in usable form at the moment people need it.** Most Canadians who face a legal problem do not know they have a legal problem. They do not know which area of law applies. They do not know what their options are. They do not know where to start.

General legal information — delivered clearly, accessibly, and without cost — addresses this gap. It does not replace lawyers. It makes lawyers more effective by ensuring that people arrive at a lawyer's office (or a legal aid clinic, or a duty counsel program) with a basic understanding of their situation.

## The Regulatory Foundation

The law society frameworks that govern this distinction are not obstacles to innovation. They are the foundation that makes responsible legal technology possible. By defining what non-lawyers can and cannot do, they create a clear space in which technology can operate with confidence.

justack.ai operates within that space. Every tool we build is designed to provide legal information and navigational guidance — never legal advice. This is not a disclaimer. It is the architecture.
