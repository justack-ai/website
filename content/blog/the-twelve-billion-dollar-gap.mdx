## The Numbers

Every year, approximately **5.4 million Canadians** experience at least one serious legal problem. These are not minor inconveniences. They are employment terminations, landlord-tenant disputes, family breakdowns, consumer fraud, immigration issues, and civil rights violations. They are the problems that reshape lives.

Of those 5.4 million people, **78% receive no professional legal help whatsoever.** They handle the problem alone, ignore it, or give up. A smaller number seek help from non-legal sources — community organizations, family, the internet — with mixed results. Only a fraction engage a lawyer.

These figures, drawn from the Canadian Legal Problems Survey and reported through CBA National Magazine and Statistics Canada, describe one of the most significant failures in Canada's public infrastructure.

## A Market Failure

It is common to frame the justice gap as a social problem — a matter of compassion and public duty. It is that. But it is also, measurably, a market failure.

Canada's legal services market is valued at approximately **$15.7 billion annually.** This is the revenue generated by law firms, solo practitioners, legal departments, and legal technology providers serving those who can access the system.

The underserved market — the legal needs of those 78% who get no help — is estimated at **$5 to $7 billion.** This is demand that exists but is not being met. These are people with real legal problems and real consequences who are not purchasing legal services, not because the need is absent, but because the market has failed to serve them.

In economics, a market failure occurs when a market does not allocate goods or services efficiently. When millions of people need a service, can be harmed by its absence, and the market does not provide it at a price or in a form they can access, that is a textbook case.

## Where the Gap Lives

The justice gap is not evenly distributed. It concentrates along familiar lines of inequality.

**Income.** Low-income Canadians are far more likely to experience legal problems and far less likely to get help. Legal aid eligibility thresholds in most provinces are set below the poverty line. A single person in Ontario earning $22,000 — barely enough to pay rent — may not qualify for legal aid. The result is a vast population too poor to afford a lawyer and too "wealthy" for legal aid.

**Geography.** Rural and remote communities have dramatically fewer lawyers per capita than urban centres. In northern and Indigenous communities, the shortage is acute. A person in Toronto has access to thousands of lawyers; a person in a northern Ontario community may have access to none.

**Language and culture.** Canadians whose first language is neither English nor French face compounding barriers. Legal information in accessible languages is scarce. Culturally competent legal services are scarcer.

**Complexity.** Legal problems cluster. The Canadian Legal Problems Survey found that legal problems rarely occur in isolation. A job loss leads to a debt problem, which leads to a housing problem, which leads to a family problem. Each additional problem reduces the likelihood of seeking help for any of them.

## The Cost of Inaction

Unresolved legal problems are not cost-free. They generate downstream costs across the public system.

The Supreme Court of Canada has recognized that lack of access to justice undermines the rule of law itself. Former Chief Justice Beverley McLachlin called it "the most important issue facing the legal system." This was not rhetoric. It was an institutional acknowledgment that the system is failing at its core function.

Unresolved employment disputes lead to lost income and reliance on social assistance. Unresolved housing disputes lead to homelessness and the costs associated with it. Unresolved family disputes lead to child welfare interventions. Unresolved debt leads to bankruptcy, mental health crises, and reduced economic productivity.

Studies in other jurisdictions have estimated that the downstream costs of unresolved legal problems — in healthcare, social services, and lost productivity — exceed the cost of providing legal help in the first place. Canada has not conducted a comprehensive cost study, but the pattern is consistent across comparable common law countries.

## What the Gap Tells Us

The $12 billion figure — the approximate total of the underserved market plus the downstream costs it generates — is not precise. It is an estimate, assembled from the best available data. The exact number matters less than what it represents.

It represents a system that works for those who can pay and fails those who cannot. It represents a market that leaves billions in demand unmet. It represents a problem that is simultaneously a social failure and an economic opportunity.

## Technology Is Not the Answer

Technology alone will not close a $12 billion gap. Legal aid funding, regulatory reform, expanded paralegal licensing, unbundled legal services, and systemic changes to court processes are all necessary.

But technology has a role. Specifically, technology can reduce the cost of delivering legal information and navigational guidance to near zero. It can help people identify their legal issues earlier, when intervention is cheaper and more effective. It can help lawyers serve more clients by automating routine work. It can match people with appropriate legal resources.

This is the premise behind justack.ai. Not that technology replaces the legal system, but that open source legal tools can reduce the friction, cost, and confusion that prevent people from accessing the system that exists.

## The Imperative

The data is not ambiguous. Millions of Canadians face legal problems without help. The costs — human, social, and economic — are enormous. The market, as currently structured, does not serve them.

**This is not a problem that will resolve itself.** It requires deliberate intervention: public investment, regulatory reform, and technology built specifically for the people the current system excludes.

The gap is $12 billion. The question is whether we treat that as an acceptable feature of the system or as a failure that demands a response.
