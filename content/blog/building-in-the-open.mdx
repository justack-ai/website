## The Decision

When we decided to open source the core of justack.ai, the license choice was not a technicality. It was a design decision with long-term consequences for who can use this technology, how they can use it, and whether the ecosystem we want to build is actually possible.

We chose **MPL-2.0** — the Mozilla Public License. Here is why.

## The Options We Considered

There are four licenses that come up in virtually every open source licensing conversation:

**MIT.** The simplest permissive license. Anyone can do anything with the code, with almost no conditions. The risk: no patent protection. A contributor could submit code and later assert patent claims against users of that code. For a project operating in the legal domain — where institutional users need certainty — this was a meaningful gap.

**Apache 2.0.** Permissive like MIT, but with an explicit patent grant. Contributors automatically grant users a license to any patents embodied in their contributions. Strong institutional credibility. But fully permissive — a competitor could fork the entire codebase, close it, and sell it without contributing anything back.

**GPL (v2 or v3).** The most prominent copyleft license. Anyone can use and modify the code, but derivative works must also be open source under the GPL. This is philosophically appealing. In practice, it creates barriers for the exact institutions we want to reach. Legal aid organizations, government agencies, and law schools often have procurement policies that restrict or prohibit GPL-licensed dependencies. Copyleft requirements introduce compliance overhead that resource-constrained organizations cannot absorb.

**Business Source License (BSL).** A time-delayed open source model: the code is source-available but not truly open source until a defined period passes. Companies like MariaDB and HashiCorp have used this model. It protects commercial interests effectively. But it is not open source — not by the OSI definition, and not in the way that matters for building public infrastructure.

## Why MPL-2.0 Wins for Access to Justice

Three factors made the decision clear.

**File-level copyleft strikes the right balance.** MPL-2.0 requires that modifications to existing files be shared back under the same license. But new files — extensions, integrations, proprietary features — can use any license. This means improvements to the core tools flow back to the community, while organizations building on top retain full flexibility. It is the Mozilla model: Firefox is open source, but the ecosystem around it includes both open and proprietary components.

**Institutional adoption.** Legal aid organizations, court systems, university research labs, and government agencies are the primary audience for justack.ai's open source tools. MPL-2.0 is OSI-approved, widely recognized, and carries the institutional credibility of Mozilla. Procurement teams understand it. Legal teams approve it.

**Ecosystem building with reciprocity.** We want other developers, researchers, and organizations to build on this work. MPL-2.0 imposes minimal friction while ensuring the commons grows. If a legal aid organization in Manitoba forks our triage engine and fixes a bug, that fix comes back. If a law school builds a proprietary research tool on top, they can — without opening their own code. The license enables both.

## The Dual Model

Open source alone does not pay salaries. The sustainability question is real, and ignoring it is how open source projects die.

justack.ai operates on a dual model:

- **Open source core.** The foundational tools — triage engines, classification systems, legal data pipelines — are MPL-2.0 licensed and freely available.
- **Commercial products.** The Navigator, Consult Connect, and professional tools built on the open source core are commercial offerings in separate files. Revenue from these products funds both the company and the continued development of the open source layer.

This is not a new pattern. **Mozilla** built one of the most important software ecosystems in the world using this exact approach: the browser engine is open source under MPL, while services and enterprise features sustain the organization. The open layer created the community. The commercial layer made it sustainable.

The key constraint is that the open source layer must be genuinely useful on its own — not a crippled version of the commercial product. If the free tools are not valuable, the community will not form. If the community does not form, the ecosystem does not exist. And if the ecosystem does not exist, the mission fails.

## What This Means in Practice

For **legal aid organizations**: you can deploy, modify, and integrate justack.ai's open source tools without licensing fees. Modifications to existing files stay open; new files you create are yours.

For **researchers**: you can study, benchmark, and build on the codebase without restrictions on publication or methodology.

For **developers**: you can contribute to the project knowing your contributions are protected, and you can build proprietary extensions in separate files without constraint.

For **the access-to-justice community**: the infrastructure is public. If Humilitas Group Limited ceases to exist tomorrow, the code remains. The tools remain. The work continues.

**That is what building in the open means.** Not just publishing code, but choosing a license that ensures the code remains useful, accessible, and safe for the people and institutions that need it most — while ensuring that improvements flow back to the commons.
