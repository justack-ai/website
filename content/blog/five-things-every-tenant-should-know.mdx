> **Disclaimer:** This article provides general legal information about residential tenancy law in Canada. It is not legal advice. Laws vary significantly by province and territory. For advice about your specific situation, consult a lawyer, legal aid office, or your provincial tenant advocacy organization. justack.ai is not a law firm.

## 1. Standard Form Leases Exist for a Reason

Several provinces — including Ontario, British Columbia, and others — require landlords to use a government-issued standard form lease for residential tenancies. These standardized documents exist to prevent one-sided contracts and ensure that both parties understand their rights and obligations.

If your landlord presents a custom lease without using the required standard form, you may have the right to request one. In Ontario, for example, tenants can request the standard lease in writing, and if the landlord does not provide it within 21 days, the tenant may withhold one month's rent.

**The standard form is not a formality. It is a protection.** Before signing anything, check whether your province mandates one.

## 2. Some Clauses Cannot Be Enforced — Even If You Sign Them

A lease is a contract, but not every term in a lease is enforceable. Provincial residential tenancy legislation overrides lease terms that violate tenant protections. Common examples of unenforceable clauses include:

- **No-pet clauses** in provinces like Ontario, where the Residential Tenancies Act generally prohibits eviction solely for having a pet (with limited exceptions).
- **Clauses requiring more than the statutory maximum for a security deposit.** In Ontario, landlords can collect only a last month's rent deposit. In British Columbia, the maximum is half a month's rent.
- **Clauses that waive a tenant's legal rights**, such as the right to file an application with a residential tenancy tribunal.

The presence of an illegal clause in a signed lease does not make it enforceable. But many tenants comply with these terms because they do not know they are unenforceable.

## 3. Rent Increases Are Regulated

In most Canadian provinces, landlords cannot raise rent by any amount at any time. Rent increase rules typically require:

- **Minimum notice periods.** Most provinces require 90 days' written notice before a rent increase takes effect. Some require more.
- **Frequency limits.** Rent can usually be increased only once every 12 months.
- **Amount limits.** Several provinces set an annual guideline for the maximum allowable increase. In Ontario, the 2025 guideline was 2.5%. In British Columbia, the formula is tied to the consumer price index.

There are exceptions. New construction, certain types of renovations, and units not covered by rent control may be exempt, depending on the province. But the baseline rule is clear: rent increases are not discretionary. They follow a legal framework.

## 4. Your Landlord Must Maintain the Unit

Landlords have a legal obligation to maintain rental units in a good state of repair, fit for habitation, and compliant with health and safety standards. This is not optional and does not depend on what the lease says.

If something breaks — heating, plumbing, electrical systems, appliances included with the unit — the landlord is generally required to repair it within a reasonable time. Tenants are not responsible for normal wear and tear.

If a landlord fails to maintain the unit, tenants in most provinces can apply to the residential tenancy tribunal for a remedy. Remedies may include rent abatement, an order compelling the landlord to make repairs, or both.

**The obligation to maintain is the landlord's. It exists regardless of the lease terms, and it cannot be contracted away.**

## 5. Proper Notice Periods Protect Both Sides

Ending a tenancy — whether by the landlord or the tenant — requires proper notice. The rules are specific and vary by province, lease type, and reason for termination.

For tenants on month-to-month tenancies, the typical notice period is 60 days in Ontario and one full rental period in British Columbia (usually one month, with notice given on the first of the month).

For landlords, the requirements are stricter. Landlords generally cannot terminate a tenancy simply because they want to. Valid grounds typically include the landlord's own use of the unit, significant renovations requiring vacancy, or non-payment of rent. Even then, specific notice periods and forms must be used.

**Verbal notice is generally insufficient.** Written notice, using the correct form where required, is the standard.

## The Larger Point

Tenant protections in Canada are substantial — on paper. The gap between law and lived experience is where access to justice matters most. Tenants who do not know their rights cannot exercise them. Landlords who count on that knowledge gap can exploit it.

These five points are not obscure legal technicalities. They are foundational. And they are the kind of information that should be freely and widely available to every person who rents a home in this country.
